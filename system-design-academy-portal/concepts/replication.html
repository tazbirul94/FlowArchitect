Replication

Keeping copies of the same data on multiple machines so reads scale, writes survive node failure, and the system stays available across regions.

Advanced Data Databases Distributed Systems

Definition & Analogy

Simple Analogy

Think of a Google Doc shared with three colleagues. When you type a sentence, it instantly appears on everyone's screen. One person's laptop dies — no data is lost because the document lives on the other machines too. The original author is the "leader"; the others are "followers" who receive updates.

Replication is exactly that for databases: the same rows/documents exist on multiple server nodes, kept in sync so reads can come from any copy and writes survive hardware failure.

A single database node is a single point of failure. If it crashes, all writes are lost and the application is down until the node recovers. Replication solves this by maintaining identical (or near-identical) copies of the data on separate machines. It also enables reads to be spread across replicas, offloading the primary and reducing read latency for geographically distributed users.

Replication is not backup. Backups are point-in-time snapshots; replicas are live mirrors updated continuously. If a bug deletes a table, that deletion also replicates to all followers in seconds. Always have both.

The Story

Real-World Story
A mid-sized SaaS company ran their entire product on a single PostgreSQL instance — 800 GB of data, 3,000 queries per second at peak. The database was on a single m5.4xlarge EC2 instance in us-east-1a. Engineers had alerts and a snapshot schedule, but no replica.
At 2:47 AM on a Tuesday, the EBS volume hosting the primary database suffered an unrecoverable I/O error. The instance went down. The last snapshot was 6 hours old. Recovery from snapshot took 4 hours and 22 minutes. The company lost 6 hours of transaction data — orders, support tickets, user activity — and was offline for nearly 5 hours. Customers churned. The CEO fielded calls all morning.
The engineering team set up PostgreSQL streaming replication: one synchronous standby in us-east-1b (same region, different AZ) and one asynchronous replica in us-west-2 for disaster recovery. They configured automatic failover using Patroni. The synchronous standby always had a transaction-complete copy of the data. Replica lag was monitored via pg_stat_replication; an alert fired if lag exceeded 100 ms.
Eight months later, the primary node's disk showed early signs of failure via CloudWatch I/O metrics. Patroni promoted the synchronous standby in 11 seconds. No data was lost. Application connections recovered within 30 seconds via PgBouncer connection pooling. Total user-visible downtime: 28 seconds. The team then configured read queries to route to the async replica, cutting primary read load by 35%.

Learn at Your Level

Beginner

Imagine you keep your diary in a notebook. One copy. If that notebook burns, the diary is gone. Now imagine you write every entry in three notebooks at the same time. If one burns, you still have two. That's replication for databases.

In databases, the leader (or primary) is the notebook you write in first. The followers (or replicas) are the copies that receive every change. Reads can come from any copy — followers handle most of them, reducing the load on the leader.

The most important thing to know: replication gives you durability (data survives crashes) and availability (the system stays online when one node fails). Every production database should have at least one replica.

Key Terms

  • Leader / Primary — The node that accepts all writes. The single source of truth.
  • Follower / Replica / Standby — A copy of the leader that receives and applies changes. Can serve reads.
  • Replication lag — The delay between a write being committed on the leader and appearing on a follower. Usually milliseconds; can grow to minutes under heavy load.
  • Failover — Promoting a follower to become the new leader when the current leader fails.
  • WAL (Write-Ahead Log) — PostgreSQL's transaction log. Streaming replication works by shipping WAL records from leader to followers.

All writes go to the Leader. The leader ships changes (via WAL records in PostgreSQL, or binary log in MySQL) to every follower. Reads can come from followers, taking load off the leader. If the leader dies, one follower is promoted.

At the intermediate level the key concepts are the synchrony model, what replication lag means in practice, and how consistency guarantees relate to where you route reads.

Synchronous vs. Asynchronous Replication

This is the most important trade-off in replication configuration. It determines whether you can lose data on leader failure.

Mode How it works Benefit Cost
Synchronous Leader waits for at least one follower to confirm it has written the change before acknowledging the client's write as committed. Zero data loss on leader crash — the promoted follower has every committed transaction. Every write incurs an extra round-trip to the follower. Write latency increases by follower network RTT (typically 1–5 ms intra-region, 50–150 ms cross-region).
Semi-synchronous One follower must ACK; others can be async. Used by MySQL Group Replication and PostgreSQL's synchronous_standby_names. Durability guarantee with one follower, without blocking all replicas. If the synchronous follower is slow or unavailable, writes block or fall back to async.
Asynchronous Leader acknowledges the write immediately without waiting for any follower. Changes stream to followers in the background. Lowest write latency. Cross-region replicas are always async (latency too high to block on). If the leader crashes before a follower catches up, those committed transactions are lost. The promoted follower has a slightly stale view.

Replication Lag

Even in asynchronous mode, followers are usually only milliseconds behind. But lag can grow:

  • Network congestion — the stream of WAL records is backpressured.
  • Heavy write burst on leader — the follower can't apply changes as fast as the leader generates them.
  • Long-running transactions on follower — in PostgreSQL, a long transaction on a replica can prevent old WAL segments from being recycled, causing the leader to retain them ("replication slot bloat").

Monitor lag with:

-- PostgreSQL: check follower lag from the leader
SELECT
  client_addr,
  state,
  sent_lsn,
  write_lsn,
  flush_lsn,
  replay_lsn,
  (sent_lsn - replay_lsn) AS replay_lag_bytes,
  write_lag,
  flush_lag,
  replay_lag
FROM pg_stat_replication;

-- Alert if replay_lag > interval '100 milliseconds'

Read-Your-Writes Consistency

A classic bug: user updates their profile photo, is immediately redirected to their profile page, and sees the old photo. The write went to the leader; the read hit a follower that hasn't applied the update yet.

Solutions:

  • Route post-write reads to the leader — For one minute after a user writes, send their reads to the leader. Simple, but increases leader load.
  • Track the client's write LSN — After a write, record the Log Sequence Number. On subsequent reads, only route to a replica whose replay_lsn >= client_write_lsn. More complex but scalable.
  • Always read from leader for user-visible writes — Profile, account settings, payment status — always leader. Analytics, timelines, feeds — can tolerate lag, use replicas.
Key Insight

Replication lag is a property of the system, not a bug. Design your application so that operations that must be consistent are always read from the leader, and operations that can tolerate eventual consistency route to replicas. The split is usually 90/10 in favour of replicas for read-heavy workloads.

Beyond single-leader, two other topologies are used in production: multi-leader (active-active) and leaderless (Dynamo-style). Each trades consistency complexity for write availability.

Multi-Leader Replication

Multiple nodes accept writes simultaneously. Each leader replicates to all other leaders asynchronously. This is the topology used for multi-datacenter active-active setups: each datacenter has a leader, and writes in each datacenter are locally low-latency.

The problem: write conflicts. Two users in different datacenters update the same row at the same time. Both writes are "committed" locally. When they replicate to each other, which wins?

Conflict resolution strategies:

  • Last-Write-Wins (LWW) — Each write is tagged with a timestamp. Higher timestamp wins. Simple, but silently loses data when clocks diverge (which they always do in distributed systems). CockroachDB and Cassandra use this by default. Dangerous for anything that matters financially.
  • CRDTs (Conflict-free Replicated Data Types) — Mathematically defined data structures that guarantee all replicas converge to the same value regardless of the order operations are applied. A grow-only counter (G-Counter), a set that only allows adds (G-Set), or a more complex structure like a CRDT register. Used by Riak, some Redis data types, and collaborative tools like Figma.
  • Application-level resolution — Conflicts are flagged and delivered to application code to merge. Amazon's shopping cart is the canonical example: the cart is a CRDT (items are only added; removal requires explicit action). The application merges concurrent carts. Most flexible; highest implementation cost.
  • Avoid conflicts by routing — If each user's data is always written to the same leader (e.g., user shard → DC1), conflicts never occur. Only works if your data access pattern allows user-level partitioning.

Leaderless Replication (Dynamo-Style)

Used by Apache Cassandra, Amazon DynamoDB, and Riak. There is no single leader; any node can accept writes. Consistency is achieved through quorums.

Given N total replicas, a write must be acknowledged by W nodes and a read must query R nodes. The invariant for strong consistency is: W + R > N.

N = 3 replicas

Strong consistency (W=2, R=2):
  W + R = 4 > N=3 → reads always overlap with at least one write ✓
  Write latency: wait for 2/3 nodes
  Read latency:  query 2/3 nodes, take latest

High availability writes (W=1, R=3):
  W + R = 4 > N=3 → consistent reads ✓
  But W=1 means only 1 node has the data before ACK — low durability

High availability reads (W=3, R=1):
  W + R = 4 > N=3 → consistent ✓
  But W=3 blocks if any node is slow

Eventual consistency (W=1, R=1):
  W + R = 2 < N=3 → stale reads possible
  Maximum availability: only 1 node needed for reads and writes

Leaderless systems use read repair and anti-entropy to heal stale replicas over time. When a read discovers a replica has an old version, the coordinator writes the newer version back. A background process continually compares replicas using Merkle trees and syncs differences.

Replication Topology Trade-offs

Topology Write Scalability Consistency Model Conflict Risk Best For
Single-Leader Leader is write bottleneck Strong (with sync replica) None — one writer Most relational workloads, OLTP
Multi-Leader Each DC writes locally Eventual; conflicts possible Yes — requires resolution strategy Multi-region active-active, offline clients
Leaderless Any node accepts writes Tunable (quorum W+R>N) Yes — version vectors required High-write NoSQL: Cassandra, DynamoDB
Architect Note

Multi-leader replication is often the wrong default. It looks attractive for geo-distribution but conflict resolution complexity is severely underestimated. Most teams building multi-region systems are better served by single-leader-per-region with a clear primary/secondary region designation and application-level fallback, rather than accepting the operational complexity of active-active writes with conflict resolution.

Raft Consensus — How Leaders Are Elected

Single-leader replication requires a mechanism to elect a new leader when the current one fails. In distributed systems, this is a consensus problem. Raft is the most widely implemented consensus algorithm (used by etcd, CockroachDB, TiDB, YugabyteDB, and Consul).

Raft in three parts:

  • Leader election — Nodes start as followers. A follower that doesn't hear from a leader within a random timeout (150–300 ms) starts an election by incrementing its term and requesting votes. A candidate wins if it receives votes from a majority of nodes. One leader per term.
  • Log replication — The leader receives client writes, appends them to its log, and replicates the entries to followers. A log entry is committed only once the leader has confirmed a majority of followers have written it. Committed entries are durable across leader changes.
  • Safety — A new leader must have the most up-to-date log of any candidate. Raft enforces this: a follower only votes for a candidate whose log is at least as up-to-date as its own. This prevents a stale node from being elected and overwriting committed entries.
Raft Election Example (5-node cluster):

Term 1: Node A is leader
  - Node A sends AppendEntries heartbeats every 50ms
  - Nodes B, C, D, E reset their election timers on receipt

Node A crashes at t=200ms

Term 2: Election begins
  - Node B's timer (170ms) fires first
  - B increments term to 2, votes for itself, sends RequestVote
  - C, D receive RequestVote — B's log is up to date → grant vote
  - B wins with 3/5 votes (majority), becomes leader for term 2
  - B sends AppendEntries (empty heartbeat) to assert leadership
  - E (partitioned) will reject term 2 writes if it starts its own election
    (term 3 → no majority → no leader elected in the minority partition)

Node A recovers at t=500ms
  - A receives AppendEntries from B with term=2
  - A sees a higher term → reverts to follower, updates its term to 2
  - A syncs its log from B (any uncommitted entries from term 1 are overwritten)

Conflict Resolution Deep Dive

Version vectors (not vector clocks) are the correct tool for tracking causality in leaderless or multi-leader systems. Each replica maintains a counter per node. A write's version vector is the set of counters at write time. On read, if version vector A is not dominated by version vector B (neither "happened before" the other), they are concurrent and require resolution.

# Simplified version vector conflict detection
from typing import Dict

VersionVector = Dict[str, int]  # node_id → counter

def dominates(a: VersionVector, b: VersionVector) -> bool:
    """Returns True if 'a' causally succeeds 'b' (a happened-after b)."""
    nodes = set(a) | set(b)
    return all(a.get(n, 0) >= b.get(n, 0) for n in nodes) and a != b

def concurrent(a: VersionVector, b: VersionVector) -> bool:
    """Returns True if a and b are concurrent (conflict!)."""
    return not dominates(a, b) and not dominates(b, a)

# Example
write_1 = {"node-A": 3, "node-B": 2}  # Write on node-A
write_2 = {"node-A": 2, "node-B": 3}  # Concurrent write on node-B

print(concurrent(write_1, write_2))  # True → conflict, must resolve
print(dominates({"node-A": 4, "node-B": 3}, write_1))  # True → 4,3 after 3,2

Multi-Region Replication Architecture

Cross-region replication is always asynchronous (inter-region latency is 50–200 ms; no production system can block writes on that). The architectural question is how to handle reads in secondary regions and how to trigger a regional failover.

# PostgreSQL multi-region replication topology (declarative config)
primary:
  region: us-east-1
  instance: db.r6g.4xlarge
  synchronous_standby_names: "standby-us-east-1b"   # intra-region sync

standbys:
  - name: standby-us-east-1b
    region: us-east-1
    az: us-east-1b
    mode: synchronous          # blocks writes; zero data loss on primary failure
    lag_alert_ms: 100

  - name: replica-eu-west-1
    region: eu-west-1
    mode: asynchronous         # cross-region; never blocks primary
    lag_alert_seconds: 30      # broader tolerance for cross-region
    read_eligible: true        # app routes EU reads here

  - name: replica-ap-southeast-1
    region: ap-southeast-1
    mode: asynchronous
    lag_alert_seconds: 30
    read_eligible: true

failover:
  tool: patroni
  dcs: etcd                   # distributed consensus store for leader election
  ttl: 30s                    # leader lease duration
  loop_wait: 10s
  promote_standby: standby-us-east-1b   # always promote sync standby first

Replication Slot Hazard

PostgreSQL replication slots prevent WAL segments from being deleted until the subscriber has consumed them. If a replica goes offline for hours, WAL accumulates on the primary. This can fill the primary's disk and cause a full outage — a cascading failure.

-- Check replication slot WAL retention
SELECT
  slot_name,
  plugin,
  active,
  pg_size_pretty(pg_wal_lsn_diff(pg_current_wal_lsn(), restart_lsn)) AS retained_wal,
  restart_lsn,
  confirmed_flush_lsn
FROM pg_replication_slots
ORDER BY restart_lsn;

-- DANGER: If retained_wal > available disk space, primary will crash.
-- Mitigation: Set wal_keep_size + monitor + drop inactive slots promptly.
-- Production rule: Never leave a replication slot inactive for > 1 hour.
SELECT pg_drop_replication_slot('stale_slot_name');  -- emergency recovery
Expert Pattern

In Raft-based NewSQL databases (CockroachDB, YugabyteDB), replication and consensus are unified: every range (shard) has its own Raft group of 3 or 5 peers. There is no separate "primary" for the whole database. Writes to a range go to that range's Raft leader, which may be on any node. This removes the single-writer bottleneck of traditional single-leader replication and enables horizontal write scaling without multi-leader conflict complexity — at the cost of higher baseline latency (every write needs a Raft quorum round-trip, minimum 1 RTT to majority).

Enterprise Use Case

Enterprise Use Case GitHub — PostgreSQL WAL-Based Streaming Replication at Scale

Context: GitHub migrated from MySQL to PostgreSQL in stages starting in 2018. By 2022 they were running 1.5+ TB PostgreSQL clusters handling millions of repository operations per day. Replication was central to their architecture for availability, read scaling, and zero-downtime schema migrations.

Setup: Each PostgreSQL cluster uses WAL-based streaming replication: one primary, one synchronous hot standby (same datacenter, different rack), and one or more asynchronous replicas used for read traffic and analytics. The synchronous standby uses synchronous_commit = on, ensuring every write is durable before the client receives an ACK. Cross-datacenter replicas use synchronous_commit = remote_write — the WAL is written to the replica's OS buffer but not fsynced, which is a middle ground between full sync and pure async.

Read distribution: GitHub's application layer (a combination of Ruby on Rails and Go services) uses a custom database connection router that sends read-only queries to replicas. When a user pushes code, subsequent reads related to that push are routed to the primary for 10 seconds (read-your-writes window), then fall back to replicas. This keeps the primary at ~30% of total query volume, with replicas handling the rest.

Lag monitoring: pg_stat_replication is scraped by Prometheus every 10 seconds. An alert fires at 100 ms replica lag (PagerDuty, on-call). At 500 ms lag, automatic throttling kicks in: the application reduces the fraction of reads routed to that replica. The replica is removed from rotation at 5 s lag. This prevents stale reads from becoming a user-visible consistency issue during heavy write bursts (e.g., a large merge or mass CI run).

Failover: GitHub uses orchestrator (later Patroni) for automated failover. On primary loss, orchestrator detects the failure via heartbeat timeout (< 30 s), confirms with Zookeeper quorum, and promotes the synchronous standby. DNS is updated automatically. Total promotion time: 10–20 seconds. Application connections reconnect via PgBouncer, which buffers new connections during the brief failover window. In two years of this setup, no single primary failure caused a customer-visible outage longer than 45 seconds.

Schema migrations: Online schema changes (OSC) run against the primary. Because replication is WAL-based, DDL changes stream to replicas automatically. Long-running migrations are validated on a replica first to confirm they don't cause excessive replication lag before running on the primary.

Common Mistakes

Reading From Replicas Without Accounting for Lag

The most frequent application-level bug: a user updates their profile (write to primary), is immediately redirected to their profile page (read from replica), and sees stale data. The replica hasn't applied the write yet — it's 200 ms behind.

Why it happens: Teams correctly add replicas for read scaling, route all reads to replicas without exception, then wonder why users see stale data.

Fix: Implement read-your-writes consistency. After a user performs a write, track the LSN and route their subsequent reads to the primary (or only to replicas confirmed to have replayed past that LSN) for a short window. PgBouncer and ProxySQL can handle LSN-aware read routing without application changes.

Never Testing Automated Failover

Teams configure Patroni or MySQL orchestrator, set up alerts, and never actually trigger a failover in production or staging. When the primary crashes at 3 AM, they discover the failover script had a bug, the DNS update took 5 minutes instead of 10 seconds, or the application didn't honour the connection timeout correctly. What should be a 20-second recovery becomes a 3-hour incident.

Fix: Schedule quarterly chaos engineering exercises. Deliberately kill the primary in a staging environment and measure end-to-end recovery time. Every component in the failover path — heartbeat detection, promotion script, DNS update, connection pool reconnect — must be validated against your RTO. Netflix's Chaos Monkey for databases: terminate a primary randomly and verify the SLO is met.

Treating a Promoted Replica as Identical to the Original Primary

When a primary fails and a replica is promoted, the new primary may be missing the last few seconds of committed transactions (in async replication). Application code that assumes "the data I just wrote is always there" will break on the promoted replica — it might be querying for rows that were committed on the old primary but not yet replicated.

Fix: Applications must be designed to handle idempotency and gracefully retry operations after a failover. Use idempotency keys for financial transactions. For async replication setups, accept that a small amount of data may be lost on primary failure and design workflows to detect and reprocess missing records (via event sourcing, audit logs, or idempotent re-sends from the client).

Interview Prep

Replication keeps identical copies of data on multiple nodes so that reads scale horizontally, writes survive node failure, and the system can serve traffic from multiple regions — with the core trade-off being how you manage consistency when followers lag behind the leader.

2-Minute Structured Answer

"Replication means keeping copies of data on multiple machines. The standard pattern is single-leader: all writes go to one primary node, and changes stream asynchronously to followers via the transaction log. Followers handle reads and are promoted when the primary fails. The key design tension is synchrony: synchronous replication guarantees zero data loss on primary failure but adds write latency equal to the follower round-trip. Asynchronous replication gives low write latency but risks losing the last few committed writes on failover. In practice, most OLTP systems use a synchronous intra-region standby for durability and async cross-region replicas for read offload and disaster recovery. The application-level challenge is read-your-writes consistency: after a write, subsequent reads must reach a replica that has applied that write, or be routed to the primary. I'd handle this by tracking LSN per request and routing reads to replicas confirmed to have replayed past that checkpoint."

Common Interview Mistakes

  • Saying "just add more replicas" without discussing the consistency implications — interviewers want to hear about replication lag and read-your-writes.
  • Confusing replication (copies of the same data) with sharding (different data on different nodes) — they solve different problems and are often combined.
  • Forgetting that the promoted replica in async replication may be missing recent writes — treating "eventual consistency" as a free lunch without discussing what happens at the moment of failover.
  • Not mentioning Raft or consensus when asked about leader election — hand-waving "a new leader is elected" without explaining how is a red flag at senior level.
